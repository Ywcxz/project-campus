Understanding Today's University

Two Digital Layers, One Gap

Before proposing a new digital layer for university life, it is worth describing the one that already exists — not as a single system, but as two systems that rarely speak to each other.

The first is administrative. Over the past two decades, most universities have adopted a Student Information System to manage enrollment and academic records, alongside a Learning Management System for coursework, and separate platforms for human resources, finance, and other institutional operations. At Visayas State University, this layer is represented by OrangeApps, which handles enrollment and grading for the university's students. These systems are official, centrally maintained, and mandatory to use for the transactions they cover.

The second layer is informal, and it is where most of university life actually takes place. Incoming students research housing through Facebook groups and inherited advice from older batchmates. Organizations run their own Facebook pages and Messenger group chats, independent of any institutional directory. Announcements move through whichever channel happens to reach a given batch first: a class group chat, a bulletin board outside a department office, a post shared and reshared until its origin is no longer traceable. Questions that have already been answered many times over get asked again, because there is no single place where the answer persists.

These two layers do not compete with each other, and they were never meant to. The administrative layer exists to process transactions the university is operationally required to track: enrollment, grades, tuition, employment records. The informal layer exists because everything else about being a student — where to live, what to eat, who to ask, what an upcoming deadline actually means in practice — still needs somewhere to happen, and no official system was built to hold it.

The result is an asymmetry worth naming directly: the parts of university life that are easiest to digitize — because they are structured, transactional, and few in number — have been digitized. The parts that are hardest to digitize — because they are social, informal, and constantly changing — have been left to accumulate wherever is convenient, one Facebook group and one group chat at a time.

Engineering Observation — 003

The administrative layer of a university is intentionally built, formally maintained, and narrow in scope. The informal layer is organically formed, unmaintained by any single party, and broad in scope — it is where housing, logistics, social life, and institutional memory actually live, without belonging to anyone in particular.

This distinction matters because it changes what “digitizing the university” should actually mean. It is not a question of replacing the administrative layer — that layer already works, for what it is designed to do. It is a question of whether the informal layer, which currently belongs to no one, can be given the same structure, persistence, and trust that the administrative layer already has, without losing the openness that makes it useful in the first place.

Design Principle — 003

Map before building. Before designing a new digital service for a university, first identify which existing layer — administrative or informal — actually owns the problem, and why no one has structured it yet. A module built without this understanding risks duplicating a system that already exists, or formalizing something that only worked because it was informal.
